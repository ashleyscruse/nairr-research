# Research Questions and Gap Analysis

## What Makes a Good Research Question

| Good fit for this program | Not a good fit |
|---------------------------|----------------|
| Can be answered with available public data | Requires months of data collection or IRB |
| Benefits from computational scale (HPC) | Can be fully solved on a laptop |
| Has a clear gap in the literature | Has been studied extensively |
| Can produce preliminary results in 5 days | Requires years of longitudinal data |
| Leads to a publishable paper | Is purely exploratory with no target venue |

## Finding Your Gap

A gap is not "nobody has studied X." A gap is a specific dimension of a well-studied problem that has been overlooked.

| Gap Type | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Population** | Studies exist for urban populations but not rural |
| **Geography** | Most work focuses on one region but ignores others |
| **Method** | Everyone uses linear models but nobody has applied deep learning |
| **Data** | Studies use single data sources but nobody has combined multiple |
| **Temporal** | Work exists at annual resolution but not daily |
| **Variable** | Studies look at one predictor but ignore interactions |
| **Scale** | Work is done at national level but not at neighborhood level |

## Using AI to Pressure-Test Your Gap

> "I found that most research on [topic] uses [common method] with [common data]. I think the gap is [your proposed gap]. Is this genuinely underexplored, or am I missing published work?"

If the AI immediately points you to papers that already fill the gap, it is not a gap. Adjust.

## Activity

Write your research question in one sentence. Then write 2-3 sentences explaining the gap. Get feedback from a peer or facilitator before moving on.
