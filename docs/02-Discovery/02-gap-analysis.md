# Stage 2: Gap Analysis and Venue Selection

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

- Identify a specific, defensible gap in the literature
- Select 2-3 target venues (conferences or journals) for your paper
- Produce a one-page research brief that anchors the rest of your week

## What is a Gap?

A gap is not "nobody has studied X." A gap is a specific dimension of a well-studied problem that has been overlooked. Gaps fall into categories:

| Gap Type | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Population** | Studies exist for urban populations but not rural communities |
| **Geography** | Most work focuses on one region but ignores others |
| **Method** | Everyone uses linear models but nobody has applied deep learning |
| **Data** | Studies use single data sources but nobody has combined multiple |
| **Temporal** | Work exists at annual resolution but not daily or hourly |
| **Variable** | Studies look at one predictor but ignore interactions between multiple |
| **Scale** | Work is done at national level but not at neighborhood level |

## Finding Your Gap

From your literature review, answer:

1. **What has been well-studied?** (Methods that everyone uses, populations that have been examined, datasets that are standard)
2. **What keeps showing up as "future work" in paper conclusions?** (Authors often tell you where the gaps are)
3. **What assumptions do existing papers share?** (Shared assumptions = shared blind spots)
4. **What would happen if you changed one variable?** (Different data, different population, different method, different scale)

### Using AI to pressure-test your gap

> "I found that most research on [topic] uses [common method] with [common data]. I think the gap is [your proposed gap]. Is this genuinely underexplored, or am I missing published work? What would make this gap more defensible?"

If the AI immediately points you to papers that already fill the gap, it is not a gap. Adjust.

## Selecting Target Venues

Your target venue determines:
- The format and length of your paper
- The audience and framing
- The review criteria and timeline
- The deadline you are working toward

### How to find the right venue

1. Look at where your key papers were published
2. Check the venue's scope (does your topic fit?)
3. Check the acceptance rate (is it realistic for a first draft?)
4. Check the timeline (when is the next submission deadline?)

### Conference vs. Journal

| Factor | Conference | Journal |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Timeline | Fixed deadlines, faster turnaround | Rolling or quarterly, slower review |
| Length | 6-10 pages typical | 15-30 pages typical |
| Review | Usually single round | Multiple revision rounds |
| Networking | Presentation opportunity | No in-person component |
| Best for | Preliminary results, new ideas | Complete, polished studies |

For a week-long project, a **conference paper** or **workshop paper** is the most realistic target. You can expand to a journal paper later.

## Activity: One-Page Research Brief

This is your Stage 2 deliverable. Write one page that includes:

1. **Research Question** (1-2 sentences)
2. **Gap** (2-3 sentences explaining what has not been done and why it matters)
3. **Data Sources** (table with dataset name, source, key variables)
4. **Proposed Approach** (1-2 sentences on method)
5. **Target Venue** (name, deadline, why it is a fit)

This brief anchors everything you do for the rest of the week. When you are deep in code on Day 3 and lose the thread, come back to this page.

## The Hard Line

**You do not move to Stage 3 until you have a confirmed gap, viable data, and a target venue.**

Without this foundation, everything that follows is a solution looking for a problem.

## Checkpoint

You move to Stage 3 when you have a completed one-page research brief reviewed by a peer or mentor.
